About

Gregory Bateson proposed that mind is not bounded by the skin but is immanent in the wider system of organism and environment—in the pathways along which information travels and the patterns in which it is organised. My research takes this seriously. I study how the form of the environments organisms inhabit and navigate—spatial, social, semantic—shapes the dynamics of cognition and behaviour, and how perturbations to these forms manifest as disruptions to health.

This leads me across two domains. In social insects, I investigate how ants modify the architecture of their built environment in response to infectious disease, restructuring the spatial networks through which nestmates interact. In human cognition, I study how the structure of meaning in language offers a window into the mind: the geometry and dynamics of semantic trajectories during speech reveal how individuals search through conceptual space, and how this search goes awry in psychotic disorders.

Across both, the connecting thread is that structure matters. The topology of a nest network shapes epidemic risk; the topology of a semantic trajectory reflects cognitive organisation. I develop tools from network science, topological data analysis, foraging theory, and dynamical systems modelling to make these structures visible and quantifiable.

Research

Architectural immunity in social insects

Social insect colonies face a fundamental tension: the dense contact that enables cooperation also facilitates the spread of infectious disease. I discovered that Lasius niger ants resolve this by actively restructuring their nest architecture when exposed to pathogens. Using x-ray micro-CT imaging to track three-dimensional nest morphogenesis, spatial network analysis, and agent-based simulations of disease transmission, I showed that pathogen-exposed colonies build nests with increased entrance spacing, reduced network efficiency, higher modularity, and chambers positioned in less central network locations—all changes that inhibit transmission. Simulations revealed that these architectural modifications synergise with behavioural self-isolation, providing enhanced collective protection.

Semantic structure as a window into the mind

When we speak, we navigate a landscape of meaning—dwelling within clusters of related concepts before switching to new semantic territory. I developed methods to characterise this process in naturalistic speech by combining contextual language model embeddings with optimal foraging theory. By casting life-story narratives as trajectories through high-dimensional semantic space, I study how individuals balance exploitation (elaborating within a topic) against exploration (transitioning between topics), and how psychosis disrupts this balance. Different symptom dimensions—positive, negative, cognitive—are each associated with distinct foraging signatures, linking clinical phenomenology to the dynamics of cognitive search.

I also use persistent homology to characterise the geometric structure of these trajectories. Psychosis symptom severity is coupled to the topology of meaning in narrative: semantic loops, voids, and linearity each track different symptom dimensions, mapping onto clinical descriptions of circumstantial speech, poverty of content, and poverty of speech. A generative model of search dynamics reproduces these topological features, providing evidence that the geometry of thought is shaped by the balance of exploitation and exploration.
