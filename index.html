About

My research investigates how organisms navigate and structure their internal and external worlds, and how these behaviours change under perturbation, with relevance for public health.

In human language, meaning traces out trajectories through semantic space. The structure of these trajectories and the dynamics with which individuals forage for semantic information provides a quantitative window into the cognition and phenomenology of psychiatric disorders, including schizophrenia and depression.

In social animals such as ants and humans, spatial structuring in the environment shapes social interactions and transmission dynamics. By modifying their built environments, animal collectives can inhibit the spread of infectious disease.

My research is carried out through the lens of behavioural ecology. I develop tools from topology, natural language processing, foraging theory, and dynamical systems modelling to characterise behavioural changes across highly diverse study systems.

Research

Semantic search dynamics in naturalistic speech

Conceptual navigation in free speech can be understood within a patch foraging framework. Efficient retrieval of semantic information balances exploitation within clusters of related conceptual information (e.g., elaborating on a topic) against exploration (e.g., transitioning to a new topic). I have developed methods to model semantic foraging in naturalistic speech, revealing that the balance of exploitation-exploration varies across psychotic symptom dimensions.

By developing techniques that combine topological data analysis with semantic embeddings, my research investigates how psychiatric disturbances disrupt how meaning is shaped into narrative arcs.

Architectural immunity in social insects

My PhD (published in Science) has shown that ant groups respond to infectious disease challenges by actively modifying the network structure of their nests to become more transmission inhibitory (e.g., more modular, less denseley connected). This work provides the first evidence of a non-human animal manipulating the structure of their built environment as a form of epidemic defense.
